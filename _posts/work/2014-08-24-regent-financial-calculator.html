On of Regent's newer products is an online financial calculator which can be customised and branded for schools and other educational institutions. It guides students through a questionnaire which takes their personal information, applies it to their preferred program and length of study, and outputs the funding available to them from grants, loans, scholarships and other sources. This enables the student to make an informed decision– Can I afford to go to school?

The product is live, but I was asked to make it work for smaller screens. I knew that the existing design would be unsuitable for use on small tablets and smartphones, so I set about researching ways to improve it.

Research

I was armed with the following assumptions:

  • Financial Aid is hard to understand
  • It is better to walk a user through a process than give them opaque questions and hope for good answers
  • Users with full-time jobs may not have time to perform adequate research, so we should give them as much information during the process as is possible
  • All users are intimidated by long forms: break up the questions into small, easy-to- undertsand chunks
  • We cannot presume the user is using a desktop or laptop

With no customers to interview, I relied on the data published by a cross-section of Regent clients to generate demographics. I found that on average:

  • 72% are female
  • 41% are minorities
  • 12% speak another language first language, then English
  • 76% are over 30 years old
  • 50% work full-time and will continue to do so alongside college
  • 20% are returning graduates
  • 21% use their phone as their primary internet browser
  • 37% own a tablet

These nuggets of information helped underline to assumptions and helped develop clear goals for creating a new design.

Analysis of the existing design

I analysed the existing design with the presumption that any where between 21% and 58% of users would not be on using a computer. I mmarked out the following technical requirements:

  Smartphone Computer Tablet
Single / multitasking Single-task Multi-task Single-task
Typical screen orientation Portrait Landscape Portrait and Landscape
Screen size 4” to 7” 11” to 27” 7” to 10”
Screen resolution (points) 320x480 to 480x800 1280x800 to 1920x1080 768x1024 or 800x1200

Next, I broke down the functional areas of screen used in the existing design and ranked them in importance:

Area Functionality Rank
Questionnaire Main interactive area 1st
Plan Progress Information Running total, funding status 2nd
Progress Bar Displays progress as percentage 3rd
Tabs Navigate to previous sections 4th
Breadcrumbs Navigate to previous subsections 5th

I also noted that the questionnaire screens were often made up of smaller subsections, and that many questions changed dynamically, depending on earlier responses. Observing our QA team test the application, it was common to see them miss a question which had appeared between two others. With anything up to 10 questions visible when entering a page, it was unsurprising that sometimnes the user would not notice until they clicked 'next' and the validation alerted them.

UX/UI Design

Sadly, I can't show the design here. Development is currently on hold, and I'm not comfortable displaying it online before it goes live.

The decison was made to show only the top three ranked areas. Additionally, I identified that the Plan Progress Information was updated so infrequently during the process. Since it was likely to become mere cognitive noise unless attention was drawn to it when it was updated, it should only be displayed on the smaller screens when an update occurred. The screen should, in fact, consist only of the progress bar and either the Questionnaire or the Plan Progress Indicator— the Plan Progress being displayed between questionnaire screens when it had been updated.

I also decided that screens with subsectons should instead be mutliple screens, one per subsection, and that only one question should be visible on entering a screen. As each question was completed, the next question would appear below it. This avoided any missed additions to the screen and focused the user on the question to hand, not one further down the screen. It still allowed the user to change previous answers.

All informational and help text was to be displayed inline, rather than hidden behind help icons. The Progress Bar would update after every question, not just between screens. A conversational tone would be adopted to make the process less intimidating, and required documentation (W2, FAFSA, etc.) would be listed before the process began to prevent users leaving the process half-completed while they searched for the required information.

The user would be informed what the process included before beginning, and how it would benefit them. When a summary screen is displayed, it informs the user why it is being shown and when they will see it again, and then indicates what will happen when they hit 'Next'.

Buttons would be descriptive: 'Start' rather than 'Begin My Future'; 'Save and Continue' rather than 'Next'. Questionnaire labels would be reworded to be friendly and indicate if they directly affect the user: 'I would like to learn' [online]/[On-campus] rather than 'Location'; My chosen program is' rather than 'Program'. Even dates would be presented in a friendlier format: '11 August 2014' rather than '08/11/14'.

I also revised the final summary screen to make it much easier to read, to highlight the most important information (Which years are funded/underfunded?), and to clearly indicate what the user should do next. Describing it any further would be an exercise in futility, so I'll wait until I'm able to post screenshots, rather than waste any more time.
